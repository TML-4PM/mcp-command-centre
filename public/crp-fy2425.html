4. Technical Uncertainty Statement

Why outcomes could not be known in advance

The core technical uncertainty across all activities was whether multi-agent AI orchestration systems could coordinate heterogeneous agents across interdependent tasks while maintaining performance, consistency, and scalability — and whether such systems could be optimised to improve human cognitive performance without inducing overload.

Prior to commencing the activities, the company reviewed publicly available scientific literature, open-source AI frameworks, academic publications on multi-agent systems, and industry documentation from leading AI platforms. While relevant concepts and partial approaches were identified, no existing method provided a means of reliably coordinating multiple AI agents with dynamic task allocation while simultaneously optimising human-AI interaction parameters for diverse cognitive profiles.

A competent professional in the field could not have determined the outcomes in advance because: (1) no established method for multi-agent orchestration at this scale and constraint profile existed; (2) the interaction between agent coordination dynamics and human cognitive performance under real-world conditions was not predictable from theory alone; and (3) the integration of signal-to-outcome mapping across heterogeneous data sources introduced combinatorial uncertainty that required empirical testing.

5. Experimental Methodology

Systematic progression of work

The company employed a systematic experimental methodology across all R&D activities, consistent with the requirements of s.355-25(1)(b) ITAA 1997. The methodology involved:

Hypothesis formulation: Each activity commenced with a written hypothesis specifying the expected outcome and the technical uncertainty to be resolved.

Experimental design: Controlled configurations were designed to isolate variables, including agent coordination models, task allocation strategies, AI involvement levels, and cognitive load parameters.

Iterative execution: Multiple experimental iterations were conducted, with each iteration informed by results from the previous cycle. Version-controlled code repositories (GitHub: TML-4PM) provide an auditable trail of experimental progression.

Evaluation and measurement: Performance was measured against defined metrics including task completion rates, response latency, output consistency, and cognitive performance indicators. Results were compared across configurations to identify effective approaches.

Conclusions: Each activity concluded with documented findings, including both successful outcomes and identified limitations that informed subsequent work or were carried forward to FY2025/26.
